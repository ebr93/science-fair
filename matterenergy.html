Matter and Energy

Everything around us is made up of matter and energy. Matter is anything that takes up space, like the toys you play with or the food you eat. Energy is the ability to do work, like the heat that makes your food cook or the light that helps you see. Scientists who study matter and energy are like explorers, trying to understand how these fundamental elements work and how they interact with each other. Their discoveries help us better understand the world around us and develop new technologies that enhance our lives.

1. States of Matter: Melting Race

Question Explored: How does different substances affect the melting rate of ice?

    Instructions:

  • 1. Place an ice cube in each bowl or cup.

  • 2. Sprinkle salt on one ice cube, sugar on another, and leave one ice cube as a control.

  • 3. Observe and time how fast each ice cube melts.

    Materials:

  • - Ice cubes

  • - Timer or stopwatch

  • - Salt

  • - Sugar

  • - Water

  • - Small bowls or cups

    Observation Questions:

  • - Did the ice cubes melt at the same rate?

  • - Which substance caused the ice to melt fastest/slowest?

  • - How do different substances affect the melting of ice?

2. Sound and Vibration Experiment

Question Explored: How do different rubber bands produce sound?

    Instructions:

  • 1. Stretch rubber bands around the tissue box or shoebox in different ways.

  • 2. Pluck each rubber band and listen to the sound it produces.

  • 3. Observe how the thickness and size of the rubber bands affect the sound.

    Materials:

  • - Rubber bands of different sizes and thicknesses

  • - Empty tissue box or shoebox

  • - Scissors

    Observation Questions:

  • - What differences in sound did you notice between the various rubber bands?

  • - How did the size and thickness of the rubber bands impact the sound they produced?

  • - What causes the rubber bands to vibrate and produce sound?

3. Dissolving Different Solids in Water

Question Explored: How do different solids dissolve in water?

    Instructions:

  • 1. Add one type of solid to each cup of water (use the same amount of water for each).

  • 2. Stir each solution and observe the speed and completeness of dissolving.

    Materials:

  • - Various solids (salt, sugar, coffee, flour, etc.)

  • - Water

  • - Transparent cups or containers

    Observation Questions:

  • - Did all the solids dissolve completely?

  • - Which solid dissolved fastest? Slowest?

  • - What factors might affect how quickly a solid dissolves in water?

4. Making a Simple Circuit

Question Explored: How does a circuit work to create light?

    Instructions:

  • 1. Cut out a small cardboard shape (e.g., a star or heart).

  • 2. Create a simple circuit by connecting the battery, lightbulb, and wires (use alligator clips or foil to connect).

  • 3. When the circuit is complete, the lightbulb should light up.

    Materials:

  • - Battery (AA or AAA)

  • - Lightbulb

  • - Wires with alligator clips or foil

  • - Cardboard

  • - Scissors

    Observation Questions:

  • - How does the circuit need to be connected for the lightbulb to turn on?

  • - What materials are necessary for the light to turn on?

  • - What happens if you break the circuit?

5. Magnetic Attraction Experiment

Question Explored: What objects are attracted to magnets?

    Instructions:

  • 1. Test the different objects by placing the magnet near them.

  • 2. Observe which objects are attracted to the magnet and which are not.

    Materials:

  • - Various objects (paper clips, coins, plastic items, wood, aluminum foil, etc.)

  • - Magnets (bar magnets, horseshoe magnets, or magnetic wands)

    Observation Questions:

  • - Which objects were attracted to the magnet?

  • - Did different magnets attract different objects?

  • - What materials seem to be magnetic and which ones aren't?
