Earth and Space

Imagine you're an astronaut floating in space, gazing down at our beautiful planet Earth. Scientists who study Earth and Space are like explorers, venturing into the vastness of space and delving into the depths of our planet. They use telescopes to observe distant stars and galaxies, satellites to map Earth's surface, and special tools to study the rocks and minerals that make up our planet.

Their discoveries help us understand how our Earth was formed, how it changes over time, and what lies beyond our planet. They study the Earth's atmosphere, which protects us from harmful radiation, and explore the oceans, which cover most of our planet and teem with life. They also investigate the Sun, which provides us with light and warmth, and the Moon, which orbits Earth and affects our tides.

1. Phases of the Moon

Question Explored: How do the phases of the moon change?

    Instructions:

  • 1. Use the marker to label eight positions (New Moon, Waxing Crescent, First Quarter, Waxing Gibbous, Full Moon, Waning Gibbous, Last Quarter, Waning Crescent) on the styrofoam/tennis ball to represent the phases of the moon.

  • 2. Use the lamp or flashlight to represent the sun. Place the ball on a table and the lamp on one side.

  • 3. Use the pencil to represent the Earth and place it between the ball and the lamp. Rotate the ball around the pencil while observing the changes in the moon's appearance.

    Materials:

  • - Styrofoam balls or tennis balls

  • - Lamp or flashlight

  • - Marker

  • - Clay or play-dough

  • - Pencil

    Observation Questions:

  • - What causes the phases of the moon?

  • - Can you describe each phase you see?

  • - Why do the phases of the moon change?

2. Solar System Mobile

Question Explored: What is the order of the planets in our solar system?

    Instructions:

  • 1. Cut out circular shapes from the cardboard or paper to represent each planet.

  • 2. Color and label each planet.

  • 3. Attach the planets to the string at different lengths (using tape or by punching holes in the cardboard and tying the string) to create a mobile.

  • 4. Hang the mobile and observe the arrangement of the planets.

    Materials:

  • - Cardboard or sturdy paper

  • - String

  • - Scissors

  • - Colored pencils/markers

  • - Pictures or drawings of planets (or draw them yourself)

    Observation Questions:

  • - What is the order of the planets from the sun?

  • - Which planet is closest to the sun? Farthest?

  • - What are some differences between the planets?

3. DIY Comets

Question Explored: How are comets formed and what are they made of?

    Instructions:

  • 1. Put dry ice (if available) in a container. If not, mix water, dirt/sand, and rocks in the container to make a mixture resembling a comet's composition.

  • 2. Use the spoon to shape the mixture into a ball or irregular shape.

  • 3. If using dry ice, observe how it sublimates and creates a tail (you may skip this step if not using dry ice).

    Materials:

  • - Dry ice (if available)

  • - Water

  • - Dirt or sand

  • - Rocks

  • - Plastic container or bowl

  • - Gloves (for handling dry ice)

  • - Spoon

    Observation Questions:

  • - What happened when dry ice was placed in the container?

  • - What did you observe in the process?

  • - How does this relate to a real comet?

4. Layers of the Earth

Question Explored: What are the layers of the Earth?

    Instructions:

  • 1. Use different colors of play-dough or clay to represent the Earth's layers (crust, mantle, outer core, inner core).

  • 2. Build a model of the Earth by layering the play-dough.

  • 3. Use the plastic knife or ruler to cut the model in half to reveal the layers.

    Materials:

  • - Play-dough or clay (multiple colors)

  • - Plastic knife or ruler

    Observation Questions:

  • - How many layers did you observe in the Earth model?

  • - Which layer is the thinnest? Thickest?

  • - What materials did you use to represent each layer, and why?

5. Water Cycle in a Bag

Question Explored: How does the water cycle work?

    Instructions:

  • 1. Fill a quarter of the bag with water. Add a few drops of food coloring if desired.

  • 2. Seal the bag and tape it to a window where it can receive sunlight.

  • 3. Observe over a few days for changes within the bag (evaporation, condensation, precipitation).

    Materials:

  • - Ziplock bag

  • - Water

  • - Blue food coloring (optional)

  • - Tape

  • - Marker

    Observation Questions:

  • - What changes did you observe inside the bag over time?

  • - How is the water cycle demonstrated in this experiment?

  • - What part of the water cycle did you observe first?
