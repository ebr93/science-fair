Organisms and Environments

Imagine you're an explorer venturing into a vast and exciting land filled with amazing creatures and breathtaking landscapes. That's what it's like to study the field of Organisms and Environments.

In this field, scientists are like detectives, carefully observing and investigating the living things around us, from tiny insects to towering trees. They explore the places where these organisms live, called habitats, and study how they interact with each other and their surroundings.

1. Plant Growth and Light Experiment

Question Explored: How does light affect plant growth?

    Instructions:

  • 1. Plant the seeds in different pots or cups filled with soil.

  • 2. Cover one pot with cardboard, one with aluminum foil, and leave one uncovered.

  • 3. Water the plants equally and place them in the same location.

  • 4. Measure and record the plant growth daily.

    Materials:

  • - Pots or cups

  • - Soil

  • - Seeds (e.g., bean seeds)

  • - Water

  • - Tape

  • - Cardboard or aluminum foil

  • - Ruler

    Observation Questions:

  • - How did the plants' growth differ under different light conditions?

  • - Which plant received the most light? Least light?

  • - What impact did light have on the growth rate of the plants?

2. Butterfly Life Cycle Observation

Question Explored: What are the stages of a butterfly's life cycle?

    Instructions:

  • 1. Observe and document the changes in the caterpillars daily as they grow and form chrysalises.

  • 2. Document the emergence of butterflies from the chrysalises.

  • 3. Record observations about the physical changes and behaviors of the caterpillars and butterflies.

    Materials:

  • - Caterpillars (can be ordered online)

  • - Habitat for the caterpillars (jar or container with food for the caterpillars)

  • - Journal or notebook

  • - Magnifying glass (optional)

    Observation Questions:

  • - What changes did you observe in the caterpillars each day?

  • - How long did it take for the caterpillars to form chrysalises?

  • - What did you notice about the butterflies that emerged?

3. Camouflage Experiment

Question Explored: How does camouflage help animals survive in their environment?

    Instructions:

  • 1. Cut out animal shapes from different colored paper or fabric.

  • 2. Choose different backgrounds (e.g., grassy, rocky, sandy) and place the animal shapes on each background.

  • 3. Use a timer to see how long it takes to find each shape on its matching background and on other backgrounds.

    Materials:

  • - Various colored paper or fabric

  • - Scissors

  • - Stopwatch

  • - Timer or watch

    Observation Questions:

  • - Which color blends best with which background?

  • - How did the camouflage help or hinder the animal shapes from being found?

  • - Why might certain colors be more effective for camouflage in specific environments?

4. Snail Habitat Study

Question Explored: What do snails need to survive in their environment?

    Instructions:

  • 1. Create a habitat for the snails using soil or sand, and add leaves or sticks for shelter.

  • 2. Place the snails in the habitat and observe their behavior and interactions with the environment.

  • 3. Measure and record any changes in the habitat, and note the snails' activities.

    Materials:

  • - Clear container or jar

  • - Soil or sand

  • - Leaves or small sticks

  • - Water

  • - Ruler

  • - Magnifying glass (optional)

    Observation Questions:

  • - What did the snails use for shelter?

  • - What did the snails eat? How did they find their food?

  • - How did the snails interact with their environment?

5. Bird Feeder Investigation

Question Explored: How does the design of a bird feeder attract different bird species?

    Instructions:

  • 1. Create different bird feeders from the plastic bottles or cartons by cutting openings for the birds to access the seeds.

  • 2. Hang the feeders in different locations (e.g., high, low, near bushes) and observe the visiting birds.

  • 3. Identify and record the types of birds that visit each feeder.

    Materials:

  • - Empty plastic bottles or cartons

  • - Scissors

  • - String or wire

  • - Bird seeds

  • - Bird field guide or internet access for bird identification

    Observation Questions:

  • - Did different bird feeders attract different bird species?

  • - Which feeder attracted the most birds? Why might that be?

  • - What did you notice about the behaviors of the visiting birds?
